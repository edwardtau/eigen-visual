Lesson 4: Complex Eigenvectors

Some matrices have no real eigenvectors at all. Instead of stretching a direction, they rotate the plane — and their eigenvalues are complex numbers. Here is how to read that directly from where Ae1 and Ae2 land.

The discriminant decides

In geometric terms: complex eigenvalues arise when the transformation rotates more than it stretches along any fixed direction, so no real vector stays on its own line after the map is applied.

Case 1 — Pure rotation

For any rotation by angle θ ≠ 0° or 180°, the discriminant is 4cos²θ − 4 < 0. The eigenvalues are cos θ ± i sin θ — a complex conjugate pair sitting on the unit circle in the complex plane.

Case 2 — Rotation + scale (spiral)

Multiplying a rotation by a scalar r doesn't change whether eigenvalues are complex — only the magnitude. If r > 1, vectors spiral outward under repeated application. If r < 1, they spiral inward. Either way, no real eigenvector exists.

A concrete example

Work through every step with A = [[1, −2], [2, 1]]. Column 1 is Ae1, column 2 is Ae2.

Now find the eigenvectors. For each eigenvalue, solve (A − λI)v = 0.

The real and imaginary parts of the eigenvector, Re = (1, 0) and Im = (0, −1), span the invariant plane in which the spiral lives. Repeated application of A rotates within that plane by arctan(β/α) = arctan(2) ≈ 63° and scales by |λ| = √5 ≈ 2.24 each step.

Reading complexity from the landing vectors

The geometric tell: if Ae1 has a large upward component and Ae2 has a large leftward component (or the mirror), the transformation is hiding a rotation — and the discriminant will be negative.
